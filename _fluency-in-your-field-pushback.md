# Thoughts on Fluency-in-Your-Field, the Numbers, and the Bridge

*Critique notes on the refined direction. The framing is right. Three places to push.*

---

## Yes — the "fluency in your field" framing is the book

The reframe is correct and important: Priya is one worked example of an AI-fluent practitioner; the book teaches a method that the reader applies in their domain. The data scientist becomes an AI-fluent data scientist. The journalist becomes an AI-fluent journalist. The marketing manager becomes an AI-fluent marketing manager. The book is *not* a re-skilling pitch. It is a fluency layer for whatever underlying competence the reader already has.

This is the version of the book that matters. It also lands the AI+1 thesis — domain identity intact, capability layered on — without the textbook ever needing to sell the program. The program markets itself. The textbook teaches the method.

Now the places to push.

---

## 1. Priya's career trajectory: be specific about which kind of comms work

You named the realistic US destination: social media manager, marketing, branding, comms. That's the right job-market read for an Indian mass-comm grad on OPT — journalism jobs are scarce and citizen-favored; brand-side comms roles pay better and sponsor visas more readily. Good.

But journalism, brand content, social media management, marketing copy, and PR are *not* one job profile. They are five jobs with five different stakes profiles:

- **Journalism / editorial.** A fabricated source is potentially defamation. Career-ending for the writer; reputation-collapse for the publication.
- **Brand content / content marketing.** A fabricated source is reputation-damaging but rarely litigated. The bigger risk is competitor misrepresentation (litigable) and audience-trust collapse.
- **Social media management.** A wrong post is fast and public. The blast-radius dimensions (reach, reversibility, identity verification) are exactly what Ch 11 teaches against.
- **Marketing copy / campaign.** A fairness failure (a campaign that misreads an audience segment) can become a public controversy. A factual error in claims about a product can be FTC territory.
- **PR / corporate comms.** A misstatement in a press release is on-the-record and quotable for the rest of the company's life.

The book's cases should cover *several* of these — not blur them. Priya works across the field. Different chapters happen in different parts of it. Ch 0's fabricated citation is journalism-shaped. Ch 11's agentic system is social-media-shaped. Ch 7's drift case might be a brand content automation. The variety is the point — it shows the reader that the *shape* of the work travels across the field, even though the *surface* changes.

This also lets the book serve the working professional reader who is in marketing or PR or social, not just the journalism-curious. If Priya only ever lives in a newsroom, the brand-side reader will feel they're reading about someone else's job. If Priya works across the comms field as her career has actually taken her, the brand reader sees themselves in at least some of the chapters.

**Concrete move:** name Priya's actual current role on the Meet Priya slide. Not "journalist." Something like *"AI Content Strategist at a US lifestyle publication, doing brand-content work that crosses into editorial."* That single sentence locates her precisely AND gives the book latitude to range across editorial, brand, and social cases without seeming to switch protagonists.

## 2. The "your field" promise must be delivered, not gestured at

Here's the failure mode: the book opens with Priya, every case is a Priya case, the introduction says "the lessons apply to your field too," and the reader never gets an active prompt to actually translate. They finish the book having learned about Priya's work.

The fix is structural. Every chapter needs an explicit translation step the reader has to *do*, not just read. Something like:

> **Translate before you move on.** Priya's fabricated source was a journalism artifact — a footnote that didn't exist. In your field, what's the equivalent? A clinical citation? A legal precedent? An economic statistic? A historical attribution? A spec reference? Name the specific artifact that, in your work, would be the load-bearing claim a fabricated AI output could take down. Don't move to Ch 1 until you've named it.

This is one block per chapter. Maybe one slide in the deck. It is the book's promise made operational. Without it, the "your field" framing is rhetorical.

The translation prompts also become the book's actual exercises — the ones the deck plan currently leaves underspecified (the "Ch X LLM Exercise" slide). The LLM exercise IS the translation step. The student writes a prompt in their domain, runs it, verifies it, brings the output back to the chapter's framework. The exercise and the translation are the same activity, named twice.

## 3. The 56% wage premium is a real headline. Cite it honestly.

The PwC 2025 Global AI Jobs Barometer numbers are credible and the right framing for the book — AI-fluent domain experts command the premium, not data-scientist-career-switchers. Use the data.

But: it's PwC's methodology, and PwC's methodology counts AI-mentioning job ads, not verified AI skills. The 56% premium is the wage difference between job postings that mention AI skills and postings that don't. That is not the same as "the same person in the same job earns 56% more by becoming AI-fluent." The figure may include composition effects (AI-mentioning postings concentrate in higher-paying industries, regions, and seniority levels) that have nothing to do with AI fluency itself.

This caveat doesn't kill the argument. It tightens it. The honest version:

> Job postings mentioning AI skills paid an average 56% wage premium in 2024 — up from 25% the year before — across industries, not just tech. The premium reflects what employers are willing to advertise for AI-fluent candidates. The methodology is an analysis of roughly a billion job ads, not a controlled study of individuals; the figure should be read as a measure of demand, not as a guarantee of personal wage gain. Even read conservatively, the demand signal is unambiguous and growing.

That paragraph cites the data, names the caveat, holds the argument. A book whose own thesis is "AI fluency is the discernment of when claims outrun evidence" cannot reproduce the unverified-statistic move with its own numbers. Citing PwC honestly is the book modeling its own method.

Same caveat applies to the NACE skills-based hiring data. The "less than 40% screen by GPA" and "two-thirds use skills-based hiring" findings are real — but skills-based hiring is uneven across industries. Tech, trades, and some operational roles have moved fast. Healthcare, law, education, finance, and government still credential heavily. The book should not paint "skills > credentials" as universal when the picture is industry-textured.

**The honest version:** skills-based hiring is rising — fastest in tech, slowest in regulated and licensed fields. The book teaches a skill. In tech and adjacent fields, that skill is the credential. In regulated fields, the skill plus the credential together is the package. Read which one you're in and apply accordingly.

## 4. The mid-career bridge — what would make it real

The Bridge product is real audience. Mid-career professionals are exactly the reader Botspeak should reach: domain identity, real work stakes, encountering AI in the workflow, not sure how to integrate it. Some will want the credential, some won't. Some will pay for the master's, some will buy the textbook and stop there.

But here's the thing the framing has not yet resolved: **what evidence of skill does the Bridge produce?**

The Plus One pathway has the co-op. That's employer-attested, real-world, hire-able evidence. It's why the Plus One graduate gets the premium — the co-op is the proof.

A mid-career bridge can't easily replicate the co-op (the student is already employed, often at a company that doesn't have a co-op infrastructure). So what's the equivalent?

Options:

- **Applied capstone with the student's current employer.** Real project, real deliverable, real employer attestation. Strong but operationally hard — requires employer cooperation and IP arrangements.
- **Portfolio of governing files.** The student leaves with their own CLAUDE.md, DESIGN.md, PROJECT.md, plus a portfolio of completed projects done against those files. Brand-portable: travels to the next employer.
- **Public capstone artifact.** A published piece — a tool, an analysis, a case study — that demonstrates the skill in a way a future employer can verify. Lower coordination cost than the employer capstone; less directly proven than co-op.

Whichever one the program picks, **the book should mirror the choice.** If the Bridge produces governing files plus a portfolio, the book's chapter-by-chapter exercises should accrete into the same artifacts. Read the book, do the exercises, finish with the same artifact a Bridge student finishes with. The book becomes a self-study path to the Bridge credential's evidence-of-skill — without the credential.

That's a strong product. The credentialed Bridge student gets the master's *and* the evidence-of-skill artifact. The self-study reader gets the evidence-of-skill artifact alone. Both can answer Ch 13's closing question. Only one has the credential next to their name. The market then decides which is enough.

## 5. Both readerships in one book, without a second protagonist

You said "no names for the sake of new names." Apply it here. The mid-career reader does not need a second protagonist. Priya at 29 with five years in journalism, encountering AI in her workflow on OPT in the US, is *structurally similar* to a 42-year-old marketing manager in Cleveland encountering AI in her workflow with no visa pressure: both are competent at the underlying work and naïve about the new tool. The book teaches the same thing to both.

What it needs is to acknowledge both readers without bifurcating the protagonist. One concrete move on the Meet Priya slide:

> Priya is 29, on OPT, working in US comms. You may be 42, with two kids and a mid-career role. You may be 51, a tenured professor about to advise a thesis whose evidence includes AI-generated outputs. The work is the same shape. The book follows Priya. The lessons translate.

Then the "translate before you move on" prompts (point 2 above) do the rest of the work. Priya is the worked example. The reader's field is the actual subject.

This means the Bridge product can use the same book. The Plus One co-op student reads it during their program. The Bridge student reads it during their master's. The non-student professional reads it on their own. All three get the same fluency. The credential is the additional artifact the program adds — not the substance the book contains.

## What this implies for the deck plan

The structure I critiqued earlier survives. What changes:

- **Slide 02 (Meet Priya):** named role is "AI Content Strategist" or similar — comms-field-spanning, not narrowly journalist. Single line acknowledging the mid-career reader.
- **Each chapter exit:** explicit "translate before you move on" block. This *replaces* the under-specified "Ch X LLM Exercise" slot — the translation IS the exercise.
- **One chapter (probably Ch 0 or Ch 7) honestly cites the 56% premium and the methodology caveat.** Model the verification habit. The book that teaches discernment should not credential-launder its own data.
- **The portfolio that accumulates across the chapters** is the artifact a Bridge student or a self-study reader leaves with. Make the artifact explicit in Ch 13 — not just "you can answer the closing question" but "you have these files, this audit history, this annotated set of outputs you produced."

---

## The one thing I'd actually want to see before more slides ship

A draft of the translate-before-you-move-on prompt for *one* chapter. Ch 0 is the natural test. Write the prompt as the reader will actually receive it on the page or slide. Hand it to three readers from three different fields (a chemistry grad student, a working nurse, a brand-side marketer). See if any of them can name what their domain's equivalent of the fabricated citation is. If two of three can — the prompt works and the book scales. If two of three can't — the prompt is too journalism-specific and needs a more abstract framing.

That's the cheap test that validates the entire "your field" thesis before slide production scales up.
