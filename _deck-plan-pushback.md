# Pushback on the Priya Deck Plan

*Critique notes on the Botspeak ~120-slide restructure. Not a sign-off. The plan is broadly right; the cracks below are worth fixing before slides get cut.*

---

## What the plan gets right (briefly, so I can spend the rest disagreeing)

The single-protagonist move is a real pedagogical upgrade — meeting Diego, Devin, Aisha, Henrik, Maya, Tessa, Ash, and Aya across eight chapters was a working memory tax most students wouldn't pay. Priya as the through-line lets the curriculum's growth ride on the protagonist's growth, which is the kind of structural coherence textbooks almost never get to. One pointer slide per Loop step and per capacity fixes a real terms-used-before-defined problem. Density per slide going down is the right trade. The MVP-first build order at the end (slides 02, 03, 06, 15, 81–91) is sound — test whether Priya carries the keystone before redoing the other 110 slides.

That's the genuine endorsement. Now the disagreements.

---

## 1. VC is not "universally legible." It's culturally familiar to a specific audience.

The plan claims VC is "universally legible without requiring domain knowledge." That is true for readers who already speak the language of memos, deal flow, Series A–C, market sizing, and partner meetings. That audience overlaps heavily with one specific demographic — MBAs, tech-adjacent professionals, business-school students — and it does not overlap with most of who Botspeak should be trying to reach. A graduate student in chemistry, a nurse practitioner, a public-school teacher, a city planner, a working journalist on a county beat — none of them will read "first-look diligence on candidate companies" and feel placed. They will feel they have walked into someone else's office.

The second claim — VC is "high-stakes outputs (a missed risk costs millions) but low-stakes-of-life (no one dies)" — is worth examining harder. The "no one dies" framing is defensible for legal/squeamish reasons (you don't want to draft a textbook in which Priya's misuse of AI kills someone). But it's not pedagogically neutral. The book argues that diligence and verification matter. Money-as-stakes is the easiest version of that argument and the one most likely to make the reader treat the lesson as optional. A nurse mis-citing a drug interaction, a teacher accepting an AI-generated assessment that mislabels a student, a journalist publishing a fabricated quote — these are the cases where the book's argument has the most teeth. Priya's missed CAGR loses the firm money. That's real. It is also the softest version of "this matters."

**Concrete move:** keep Priya as the spine *and* let one or two chapters break the protagonist to use a different kind of stakes — explicitly. The book could frame this as: "The Loop and the Capacities are domain-independent. Priya is one practitioner. Here is what they look like for someone whose missed citation is a medication error." One vignette in Ch 6 or Ch 11 that breaks frame and shows a non-VC failure costs the plan maybe 4 slides and saves the book from looking like it was written for one audience.

## 2. The compression of eight named cases to one protagonist loses something the plan should name.

The current eight cases (Diego, Devin, Aisha, Henrik, Tessa, Ash, Aya, Maya) gave the book range. Different fields. Different stakes. Different kinds of failure mode. Recasting all of them as Priya scenes means: the reader learns that fluency looks like Priya's work week. That is fine if you accept "Priya = exemplar of fluency." It becomes a problem if you wanted the reader to learn that fluency takes different shapes in different fields. The plan doesn't name this trade-off; it should.

In particular, examine Ash carefully. The original Ash case is structurally important because it shows an agentic system taking an *irreversible* action that a human couldn't undo. Recast as "the firm's deal-flow agent dropped a candidate the partners would have wanted to see," the irreversibility test softens — a dropped pitch can be revisited; a deployed agent action in financial services or operations often cannot. The Ch 11 lesson about agency is partly about reach and reversibility. The Priya version may not test reversibility hard enough. Worth examining.

## 3. The career arc has a time-handling problem.

Priya is six months in at Ch 0, two years in at Ch 8, mid-level by Ch 13. The plan is silent on how long the book covers. Three options:

- **Calendar real-time.** The chapters happen over Priya's two-year arc. Then Ch 1–7 cover a year-and-a-half between Ch 0 (month 6) and Ch 8 (month 24). That's a lot of compressed implied time and the chapters lose their week-to-week texture.
- **Compressed teaching time.** The chapters happen over a few months. Then Priya's "two years in" at Ch 8 contradicts Ch 0's "six months in" and the time stamps are fictive scaffolding rather than continuous narrative.
- **Mixed.** Some chapters are weeks apart in Priya's life; some are months apart. The book is loose about it.

Option 3 is fine *if you say so*. Right now the plan doesn't say. Pick one and put it on the Meet Priya slide. "Two years, told in scenes" or "Six months, taught as if each week was its own chapter" or "An arc from junior to mid-level, time compressed for teaching." Otherwise the careful reader will notice and the inconsistency will read as carelessness.

## 4. Priya's identity in a famously homogeneous field is either an asset or a missed beat.

Priya Banksy — South Asian woman, mid-level VC analyst at a Boston firm. VC is famously white-male. There is friction between the protagonist and the setting that the book can do one of two things with:

- **Engage with it.** Name that Priya is one of two women in associate meetings; let one of the case stakes turn on a partner not taking her objection seriously the first time; thread it into the diligence chapter where authority and information are both real verification conditions. This sharpens the book's argument about who pays the verification cost — sometimes the answer is "the person whose objection had to be raised twice."
- **Ignore it.** Priya is just an analyst; her demographic is set-dressing; the cases land on competence not identity.

Option 2 is safer and easier to write. Option 1 is bolder, more honest, and makes Priya a fuller character. The plan should pick a side. If the book is going to choose a non-default protagonist, doing so without ever naming the friction is the worst of both worlds — you get credit for the choice and lose the chance to use it.

## 5. The vocabulary slide (slide 03) violates the plan's own "one idea per slide" principle.

The plan correctly argues for one pointer slide per Loop step (slides 16–20). The same logic should apply to vocabulary. Twelve terms on one slide, each as a hook, is the kind of dense reference slide the plan otherwise tries to escape. The student's eye won't follow twelve hooks; it will skim them and forget them by slide 15.

Better moves:

- Split into three vocabulary slides grouped by theme — Loop steps (5 terms), Capacities (3 terms), Modes & Disclosure (3 terms), Stance terms (1–2 terms).
- Or: cut the vocabulary slide entirely and let each term land in scene when it first matters. Trust the chapters.
- Or: move the twelve-term scaffold to a printed-handout reference card that students keep next to the deck and pull from when needed, freeing the slide budget for scene work.

The MVP plan says to build slide 03 first. I'd argue slide 03 is one of the weakest slides in the plan — build it last, after the rest forces you to discover what scaffolding is actually missing.

## 6. The Ch 0 / Ch 8 parallel is the structural payoff of the whole rewrite. Name it.

Both chapters happen on Tuesdays. Both feature Priya facing a brief. Both involve verification. The pre/post fluency contrast is built into the structure: six-months-in Priya fails on Tuesday, two-years-in Priya succeeds on Tuesday, same protagonist, same kind of task, 18-month skill differential. This is a powerful pedagogical move and the plan does not call it out.

**Concrete move:** make slide 81 explicitly reference slide 06. Sub-header like "Tuesday, eighteen months later." Let the deck visually mirror — same setup, different outcome. Instructors will use this. Students will remember it. Right now the parallel is implicit; making it explicit costs nothing and gives the rewrite its strongest single artifact.

## 7. The closing question shows up at the end. It should be a recurring beat.

The book's actual claim — "What specifically did the AI do, and what did you do that it could not?" — appears on slide 128, in Ch 13. That is the test the entire course trains for. Landing it cold at the end means the student meets the test as a closing flourish rather than as the standard they've been practicing against the whole time.

**Concrete move:** seed the question at slide 12 (Ch 0 closing) — Priya cannot yet answer it. Resurface it at the end of every chapter as a one-line check ("Can Priya answer the closing question yet? Almost — she can now name X but not Y."). By Ch 13 the question is familiar; the answer is the punchline. This costs about one line per chapter exit slide. The payoff is that the closing question becomes a recurring assessment standard, not a one-time delivery.

## 8. The "Ch X LLM Exercise" slide is the most underspecified element of the plan.

Every chapter ends with one. The plan does not say what they look like, how they vary, what they ask the student to do, or how they connect to each other. This is the actual practice the book teaches. If each is one slide of throwaway, the whole pedagogy weakens.

Questions the plan should answer before slide production starts:

- Is each exercise a single prompt the student copy-pastes into Claude?
- Does the exercise sequence build — Ch 1's exercise output becomes input to Ch 2's exercise?
- Is the exercise meant to be done in class with an instructor watching, as homework, or both?
- What is the verification step inside the exercise — how does the student know whether they did it well?
- Does Priya do the exercise on the slide before the student does, or alongside?

Right now these slides read as "we'll figure it out." That is the most consequential ambiguity in the plan. Resolve it before MVP build, not after.

## 9. The keystone test for Ch 8 is missing.

The plan calls Ch 8 "the chapter that earns Part I." Good framing. But it doesn't name the test. Here is one:

> Ch 8 earns Part I if a reader who has done chapters 0–7 can predict every move Priya makes on her Tuesday before reading what she actually does.

If they can't, the prior chapters didn't teach the Loop. That's the standard. Use it as the validation criterion when MVP slides 81–91 get reviewed. If a test reader cannot anticipate Priya's 1:14 PM loop-back move, fix Ch 5 or Ch 6, not Ch 8.

## 10. Modularity for instructors is implied but not stated.

121 slides is a substantial commitment. An instructor on a half-semester schedule will want to know: can I teach Part I in 6 weeks (slides 5–91, 87 slides), or do I need to cut? Can I skip Ch 11 if my course doesn't cover agentic systems? Is there a "Botspeak in 30 slides" sub-deck for one-off lectures?

The plan implies the chapter structure supports modularity. It doesn't say. **Concrete move:** add a one-paragraph "Teaching configurations" section to the plan naming three: full course (all 121), Part I only (slides 1–91), short course (a named subset of ~30 for a single lecture or workshop). Instructors will ask. Better to answer in the plan than in the inevitable follow-up.

## 11. A specific worry about Slide 12 — the Ch 0 LLM exercise.

The plan says: "Generalize from 'your role' to a worked Priya exercise students follow." This is the first LLM exercise the student sees. It sets the tone for all twelve that follow. The pre-rewrite version asked the student to do their own work in their own role. The Priya version centers Priya's role.

Question worth asking: does the student do the exercise *as* Priya (pretending to be a VC analyst) or *like* Priya (running her process on their own work)? The first is alienating for the chemistry grad student. The second is the right move but harder to scaffold on one slide.

**Concrete move:** slide 12 explicitly invites the student to pick: do it Priya's way on her data, or run the Priya process on something from your own week. The "or" itself is teaching — fluency is method-portability.

## What I'd actually build first

The MVP plan picks slides 02, 03, 06, 15, 81–91. I'd revise to:

1. **Slide 06** — Priya's Tuesday, six months in. The single most load-bearing slide in the rewrite. If this case doesn't grab, nothing downstream does.
2. **Slides 81–91** — Ch 8 keystone. The mirror to slide 06. Build them together so the parallel structure (point 6 above) actually surfaces.
3. **One Ch X LLM Exercise slide, fully designed** — pick the hardest (Ch 6 Discernment or Ch 11 Agency) and design it end-to-end. Resolve the ambiguity in point 8 before producing twelve more.
4. **Slide 02** — Meet Priya. After the keystone is built, you'll know what the protagonist actually needs to be.
5. **Slide 128** — The closing question. Build it before any other Ch 13 slide. Then walk backward and seed it through the deck per point 7.

Skip slide 03 (vocabulary scaffold) for the MVP. It's not load-bearing and the plan's instinct on it is probably wrong (see point 5).

## The biggest open question

The plan does not name what failure would force a redesign. So:

**What would force a rewrite of the rewrite?** If five test readers from outside finance read Ch 0 and Ch 8 back-to-back and cannot articulate what Priya did differently between the two — and cannot map "what Priya did differently" onto their own work — then the Priya scaffold did not teach. The protagonist absorbed the lesson rather than transmitting it. That's the failure mode worth watching for.

If three out of five test readers say "I get what she did but I couldn't do it on my own work," the protagonist is doing too much heavy lifting and the chapters need more abstraction. If three of five say "yeah, I'd do this on a project I'm in," the protagonist is doing what protagonists are supposed to do.

That test goes before MVP slide production, not after.
