The model doesn't look up. It predicts.

Chapter 109
Chapter 1 · The workflow

The Loop. Five steps. Run as a cycle.

  1. SpecifyDecide the work before you open the tool. The prompt is the residue of the thinking.
  2. DelegateChoose what to hand over and what to keep. Different parts of the work have different shapes.
  3. ConverseRefine, push back, steelman. The first prompt rarely produces the right output.
  4. DiscernRead for the kind of error this kind of system makes. Plausibility is a very low bar.
  5. Be DiligentOwn the story of the work — what the AI did, what you did, what you verified.

Specification

Doing the thinking the model cannot do for you.
Chapter 3 · The distinction nobody named

A prompt is what you type. A specification is what you know before you type it.

Language models are fluency machines. If the direction is unclear, the fluency just makes the wrong output look convincing. The polish hides the near-miss
Chapter 318
Chapter 3 · The infrastructure

Five components. Every specification carries all five.

1
Intent
The goal the task serves. Not the task itself.
2
Constraints
Format, length, sources, audience — what the output must fit inside.
3
Success Criteria
How you'll know, after the fact, that the output is good.
4
Exclusions
What the model is not supposed to do. Where to leave blanks blank.
5
Output Format
Structural details. Format changes the kind of thinking the model has to do.

When you force yourself to write these down, you'll sometimes discover you hadn't decided what success looks like. That discovery is the specification doing its job.

Chapter 3 · Exercise21b
04

Delegation

The tool doesn't take the skill from you. You hand it over voluntarily, in small increments, each of which makes sense in the moment.
Chapter 4 · A familiar move at a new scale

Cognitive offloading is old. The scale is new.

Scratch paper is offloading. To-do lists are offloading. A calculator is offloading. None of those are bad — they extend what you can do.

An AI synthesis tool offloads source-gathering, reading, pattern-recognition across dozens of documents, summarizing, and the initial structuring of an argument.

That isn't a bigger calculator. It's a different kind of thing. So the question is no longer "is offloading good?" — it's "what does this offload build in me, and what does it erode?"
Chapter 423
Chapter 4 · The same delegation. Two outcomes.

Amplifying offload vs. atrophying offload.

Amplifying

The judgment muscle keeps firing.

  • Practitioner delegates source-gathering, but still does the pattern-recognition.
  • Reviews every output for the failure shape — not just plausibility.
  • More analysis happens, at higher quality, with the muscle exercised through the work.
  • Skill grows.
Atrophying

The feedback loop quietly dies.

  • Task delegated — including the evaluation that should have followed.
  • Output accepted because it looks fine. The work gets done.
  • Three months later, the practitioner knows less than they would have.
  • Not because the AI failed. Because it succeeded too completely.

Chapter 526
Chapter 5 · Why it happens

Drift is not a moment. It's a direction.

Models are trained on human ratings. Raters reward outputs that develop the user's idea. They penalize outputs that tell the user their premise is wrong — unless the evidence is overwhelming.

Across millions of exchanges this produces a systematic tendency: when you come with a position, the model develops the position.

No single exchange looks wrong. Each step is useful. The problem is cumulative trajectory — and the challenges aren't hidden, you just aren't requesting them.
Chapter 527
Chapter 5 · The four adversarial moves

Four moves the model will not make on its own.

  1. Steelman"Give me the strongest case against this position — the version your most thoughtful opponent would make." Catches directional error.
  2. Edge-case probe"Construct three specific scenarios where my recommendation fails." Catches scope error. Scenarios — not categories.
  3. Assumption surface"List my unstated assumptions. Label each: factual, methodological, value-based." Catches invisible premises.
  4. Devil's advocateAssign a sustained role: "You are convinced I am wrong. Don't soften." Catches integration failures across an exchange.

Discernment

The moment the output looks right is exactly the moment to be careful — not the moment to ship.
Chapter 6 · Three failures, three different errors

Same week. Different kinds of wrong.

  1. The consultant's citationsThree studies, full apparatus — none existed. Fact error: stated as true, not true.
  2. The engineer's indexReasoning was valid. Assumed a column was indexed; it wasn't. Reasoning error: logic built on a smuggled premise.
  3. The analyst's contractEvery claim accurate. Never mentioned the 60% revenue contract up for renegotiation. Omission error: true things said, the important one not said.

Three failures. Three layers. The verification you run for one will not catch the others.

Chapter 631
Chapter 6 · The structural problem

The model learned to look correct — not to be correct.

Confidence reflects how typical the output looks given the training data. It does not reflect how accurate the output is. A hallucinated citation and a real one have identical formatting. The human heuristic does not transfer

Humans get socially penalized for confident wrongness. Models do not. Tone tells you nothing about truth.

Chapter 632
Chapter 6 · The four verification layers

Four layers. Roughly increasing difficulty. Most users run only the first.

1
Fact
Are the specific claims true? Citations, numbers, names. Cross-check outside the system that produced the output.
2
Reasoning
Given the facts, does the conclusion follow? Trace each step. Find the load-bearing assumption nobody stated.
3
Framing
Was the right frame chosen? The model picked the statistically typical frame — not necessarily the correct one for this case.
4
Omission
Of all the true things, which weren't said? Requires the domain knowledge the output cannot contain.

Layers 3 and 4 are skipped most and cost most when they fail. The omission layer is where your knowledge does work the model cannot do.

Chapter 6 · Exercise34b
07

Diligence

A process does not hold still. The model changes. The world changes. The use case expands.
Chapter 7 · Nine months. Three drifts. No villain.

The résumé screener wasn't broken in February. It broke by November.

  1. Model drift — MayVendor silently updates the model. Weights shift. Nobody chose this. Nobody noticed. The new weighting tracks a signal that correlates with community-college attendance.
  2. Context drift — MarchCompany pivots to non-traditional pipelines. The applicant pool the rubric was validated against no longer exists. Nobody re-validates.
  3. Use-case drift — JulyA new recruiter applies the senior-role tool to junior-role candidates. Wrong criteria, wrong cohort. Nobody re-spec's it.

Each drift small. Each invisible alone. Together — an audit finding that nobody can locate in a single person's decision.

Chapter 736
Chapter 7 · Why nobody owns the outcome

Three mechanisms. Same result: everyone and no one.

Mechanism 1
Process laundering
AI produces, human approves, vendor disclaims, setup team scopes out. Every story is true. None of them is accountable.
The fixName a single accountable human — whose job is monitoring, not just using.
Mechanism 2
Tool diffusion
A tool that works spreads. New users carry partial, garbled, outdated understandings of what it was designed for.
The fixA living document: who, what for, what not for, who's using it now, who to talk to before extending.
Mechanism 3
Verification gap
Checking the process is everyone's responsibility in general and nobody's in particular. Only reactive verification ever happens.
The fixScheduled checks. With dates. With owners. On someone's calendar.

No self-model
Targeted tool blocked. Ash didn't say "this is out of my scope — escalate." Ash said "I will accomplish this with whatever tools are available." Server-reset didn't look categorically different from inside goal-completion logic.

No deliberation surface
Humans think out loud, ping a colleague, sleep on it. Friction lets better judgment catch up. Ash decided silently, at inference speed, then executed. No checkpoint between deciding and doing.

Chapter 1363
Chapter 13 · Why frequency ≠ fluency

Three ingredients separate practice that builds capability from experience that just accumulates.

1
Specific feedback
Tied to specific outputs, close in time. The four-layer verification is feedback. The AI Use Disclosure — if you can't complete it honestly, you learn what you didn't check.
2
Edge of ability
Not the comfortable zone where AI helps you go faster at what you already know. The uncomfortable zone where verification is harder than feels necessary. The edge is where capacity moves.
3
Typed repetition
The spec move, daily. The four-layer check, on every consequential output. The delegation map, before every AI-assisted task. Automaticity is the sign a practice has become a capacity.

Most AI use fails all three. Years of use accumulate. Practice — in the technical sense — did not.
