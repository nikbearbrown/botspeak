# Thoughts on Priya-as-AI+1-Journalist

*Critique notes on rewiring the Botspeak deck's protagonist into the AI+1 frame. The age change and the journalist switch are both upgrades. The wiring still needs work.*

---

## What I'd endorse without argument

**29 over 23.** A 23-year-old fresh grad making a citation mistake reads as "still learning." A 29-year-old journalist with five-plus years in the work making the same mistake reads as "should have known better, and almost didn't catch it." The second framing is the book the textbook is trying to be. Fluency is what older-and-more-experienced practitioners cultivate; literacy is what fresh grads have. The age change shifts Priya from literate-junior to mid-career-practitioner-on-her-way-to-fluent — which is where the actual readership of this book lives.

**Journalist over VC analyst.** Three independent gains:

1. *Universal legibility actually achieved.* Most readers have read journalism. Far fewer have done VC. The "Boston-based VC analyst" persona was legible to a specific class of MBA-adjacent reader and opaque to the rest. A Mumbai-trained journalist working in US media is legible to graduate students in every field.
2. *Real stakes, named honestly.* A journalist publishing a fabricated source is potentially career-ending — defamation, retraction, public trust collapse. That's a much sharper version of "missed verification" than "the firm loses a deal." The earlier pushback that VC made the book's argument soft is now answered.
3. *The wrong-arrow problem becomes legible.* "Journalism → CommTech Engineer" is laughably wrong in a way "Finance → FinTech Engineer" is not. The journalist persona makes the AI+1 thesis (the identity stays, AI layers on) visible in a way the VC persona obscured.

**No names for the sake of new names.** This is the right rule. The temptation to make Priya the protagonist of every case is the temptation to flatten the book. Some characters earn their continued existence because they teach something Priya — by virtue of her specific context — cannot. Apply the rule case by case. See below.

## Where the framing needs to be deliberate, not assumed

### 1. The book is not the AI+1 brochure.

If Priya is tightly bound to AI+1 — applicant, current student, recent alumna whose every move references the program — the textbook starts to read as program collateral. A reader at MIT, or self-studying, or working at a firm that does its own training, will smell the marketing pitch and back away. That is a real failure mode.

The fix is structural, not cosmetic. Pick one:

- **Priya is an AI+1 graduate, the book is set 1–2 years after.** The program is mentioned in her bio, never referenced as the cause of her fluency. The book teaches fluency as a portable practice. AI+1 readers see themselves in her; non-AI+1 readers see a working practitioner whose origin doesn't matter to the lesson.
- **Priya is a current AI+1 student, and the book is openly the program's pedagogical text.** This is honest and the book reads as a teaching text used inside AI+1 — like a textbook used in a specific curriculum without claiming to be universal. Limits the audience but does not pretend.
- **The book is dual-track.** Priya is an AI+1 student in the front matter and Ch 0; she becomes a working practitioner from Ch 1 forward and the program never resurfaces. Costs one sentence to set up; opens the audience back up.

Option 1 is the strongest if the goal is a textbook with reach. Option 2 is the most honest if the goal is the AI+1 program's signature text. Option 3 is the soft compromise. Pick deliberately. Don't drift between them in different chapters.

### 2. The visa angle adds depth and threatens to dominate.

OPT runway, the 36-month STEM clock, three H-1B lottery shots, the difference between sponsorship and going home — these are real stakes for an international graduate student. They make Priya a more vivid character. They also threaten to make the book about *immigration anxiety as a productive forcing function* as much as it's about AI fluency.

Test: imagine an American-born reader, a working professional in Cleveland or Houston, opening Ch 0. Is the visa runway the first thing they learn about Priya? If yes, the book has just told them "this is for someone else." If the visa runway lands later — as one specific way Priya's career stakes are configured — it adds texture without taking over.

**Concrete move:** the visa stakes get one sentence in the Meet Priya slide and one chapter (probably the diligence or delegation chapter, where stakes-calibration is the lesson). Otherwise off-stage. The book is about fluency. The visa is a fact about Priya, not the topic.

### 3. The "internationally iconic" framing solves one problem and creates another.

The AI+1 marketing pitch needs a recognizably international persona — that's the audience the program is differentiating itself for. A Symbiosis Pune-trained journalist applying for STEM-OPT runway is iconic in a way that lets the dean picture the student.

The textbook has a wider audience. American-born journalism students. Working professionals already in US newsrooms. Non-journalism students who care about the methodology. If Priya reads as *exclusively* the AI+1 international target persona, those other readers won't see themselves.

The trick is to make Priya specific enough to be real (29, Indian, Symbiosis-trained, working in US media on OPT) and structurally typical enough to be the spine (her work — verification, specification, discernment, diligence — is what every reader's work also requires).

This is solvable but requires care. The Ch 0 scene shouldn't open on "Priya, the international student" — it should open on "Priya, the journalist working on a brief," with her biographical detail entering as it becomes relevant. The reader meets the work first, the demographics second. That ordering is what makes the protagonist universal-via-specificity instead of representational-set-dressing.

### 4. The 29-year-old journalist's failure profile is different from the 23-year-old VC's.

The Ch 0 case (the citation that wasn't) translates almost too well. A working journalist at 29 letting an AI-generated source slip past her — that's career-defining. But: at 29 with 5+ years of journalism experience, would she *really* not have caught it? The earlier 23-year-old version had the legitimate excuse of inexperience. The 29-year-old version needs a sharper account of how the failure happens to someone competent.

The honest answer is: she's competent in journalism craft and not yet fluent in AI verification. That gap — the practitioner who is excellent at the underlying work and naïve about the tool that just entered the workflow — is exactly the gap the book is teaching to. Make Ch 0 about that specific gap. Don't soften it into "she was tired" or "the deadline was tight." The lesson is sharper if it lands as: a competent journalist who has not yet built the AI-verification habit, ships a piece with a fabricated source, and discovers that competence in the underlying work does not transfer automatically to the new layer.

That framing also generalizes — every reader is competent at something and naïve about the AI verification layered onto it.

## "No names for the sake of new names" — applied case by case

Going through the existing supporting cases:

**Citation That Wasn't (Ch 0, was Diego).** Becomes Priya. The case is foundational; she has to own it. **Diego retires.**

**Calendar arithmetic error (Ch 2, was Devin).** Could be Priya — but her field is journalism, not deal-flow code. The case as written was about AI-generated code with a fiscal-year edge case. In journalism, the equivalent is something like: an AI-generated date conversion in a piece about an international story (e.g., a UTC-vs-local-time error in a breaking news brief), or a metadata error in a data journalism piece. Both work. **Translate to Priya** but specify what the journalism-equivalent failure looks like before slide production. Don't fudge it.

**Three bad prompts (Ch 3, was Aisha).** Becomes Priya summarizing something — a court ruling, a transcript, an industry report. Translates cleanly. **Priya.**

**Cobalt objection found by colleague (Ch 5, was Henrik).** This case is *specifically* about a colleague catching what the protagonist missed. The case requires *two* characters in a relationship: the one who built the case and the one who steelmanned it. If both are Priya (she builds, then she steelmans herself), the lesson softens — the whole point is that another mind found what hers didn't. **Keep the second character.** Name them appropriately (a senior editor, a fact-checker, a sources desk colleague). The character earns their place because the lesson is relational.

**Delegation map (Ch 4) and Tuesday keystone (Ch 8, was Maya).** Both become Priya. The Tuesday keystone is *the* keystone — she has to own it. **Priya.**

**Inherited automation (Ch 9, was Tessa).** Two possibilities:
- Priya inherits an automation built by the previous AI editor at her publication. Lesson lands.
- An earlier-in-career Priya inherits from a senior; or a different character (Tessa, or a renamed equivalent) inherits the automation and Priya is the reader observing.

I'd argue **Priya inherits.** The lesson is about supervisory responsibility for systems you didn't build. That's exactly the position a mid-career journalist takes when joining a newsroom or a brand's content team. It's also exactly the position an AI+1 graduate is sold on — you walk into an organization that already has AI tooling running and you become the responsible adult in the room.

**Agentic system / irreversibility (Ch 11, was Ash).** This is the hardest translation. The original case turned on an autonomous agent taking an irreversible action a human could not undo. For Priya the journalist, what's the journalism equivalent?

Options:
- An automated headline generator publishes a draft headline to social before the editor reviews. Reach is real (social spreads fast), reversibility is partial (you can delete but the screenshot exists), identity verification matters (was it actually under the publication's name).
- An automated translation pipeline pushes a story to a foreign-language wire service with an error that misrepresents the source's quote.
- An AI moderation agent on a comments section bans a real source's account before a human review.

All three work. The strongest is probably the social-publish case — most universal, most legible, most clearly tests the four blast-radius dimensions (reach, reversibility, identity verification, escalation).

**Either way: this case is Priya's, but the agentic system is a real piece of journalism infrastructure, not invented set-dressing.** Specify it before slide production. Don't write the lesson abstractly.

**Verification under autonomy (Ch 12, was Aya).** Becomes Priya. The case is about the human-in-the-loop reviewing a flagged item. A journalist reviewing an AI-flagged story for accuracy, fairness, or sensitivity translates naturally. **Priya.**

**Net character count for the new deck:** Priya + one named colleague in Ch 5 (the steelmanner) + possibly one named predecessor in Ch 9 (the prior automation builder). That's a much cleaner cast than the eight-name original *and* than the all-Priya overcorrection. The book has a spine and one or two supporting characters who earn their place.

## What this implies for the deck plan I critiqued earlier

The structural moves all survive. Single protagonist (with named supporting cast where earned). Ch 0 / Ch 8 Tuesday parallel. Lower density. Pointer slides for Loop steps and capacities. MVP build order.

**What changes:**

- Slide 02 (Meet Priya) is now meaningfully harder to write. The bio has to do real work: 29, Indian, Symbiosis Pune, working in US media, AI+1 alumna (or current student — see point 1 above), her current role, her current stakes. The slide must establish the protagonist as a working professional, not as a demographic case study.
- Slide 06 (Tuesday, six months in) needs translation. Six months into *what*? Her co-op at a US publication? Her first job out of AI+1? Her first staff position? Pick one. The choice frames whether the book's arc is "AI+1 student becomes fluent" or "AI+1 graduate becomes fluent" or "working journalist learns AI fluency in the field."
- Each case translation has to be specified in journalism terms — what specifically did the AI do, what specifically did Priya not catch, what specifically was the consequence. The old VC cases had concrete numbers (the 8.4% CAGR). The new versions need equally concrete journalism artifacts (the missing source's name, the misattributed quote, the algorithmic headline that ran for 47 minutes before someone caught it).

**What this saves:** the work of translating eight named cases into one named protagonist's career is the work the book has to do anyway. Doing it deliberately, with the AI+1 frame as the through-line, makes the protagonist more consequential — not less.

## The biggest open question I'd resolve before MVP slide production

**Where is Priya in her career on the day Ch 0 opens?**

The choice has cascading effects. Three options:

1. **Six months into her AI+1 co-op at a US publication.** She is a master's student. The textbook is set in the AI+1 program. Audience: AI+1 students. Risk: marketing brochure.
2. **One year post-AI+1, at her first staff job at a US publication or brand.** She is a working professional, OPT clock running. The textbook is set in the field. Audience: AI+1 alums plus the wider professional audience the book hopes to reach. Risk: the program's role becomes invisible.
3. **Three years into journalism, never went to AI+1, encountering AI in her newsroom in real time.** She is a working journalist, fully professional, no program affiliation. The textbook is universal. Audience: any reader. Risk: the AI+1 angle disappears entirely and the book doesn't serve the program.

The right answer depends on what the book is for. If it's the textbook for AI+1, go with #1 or #2. If it's a textbook that AI+1 happens to assign, go with #2 or #3. If it's a trade textbook with broad reach, go with #3.

Pick before slide 02 ships. Not after.

## One small thing

The proposal closes "Want me to swap Priyanka for Priya in the OPT slide paste-ready block, with the updated…" — that's a different deck (AI+1 marketing), not this book. The Botspeak Priya and the AI+1 marketing Priya should be the same person across the brand, *and* the textbook needs to function without the marketing context. If a reader has never seen the AI+1 brochure, the Botspeak Priya should still hold up. Test that on the Meet Priya slide: does it make sense without the AI+1 pitch sitting next to it? If no, pull the program reference back into "one sentence in her bio" and let the work do the rest.
