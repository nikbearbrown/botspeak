## 2. The book concept, in one paragraph

This handbook teaches AI fluency to recent graduates entering AI-saturated workplaces, by giving them a structured workflow (the Loop) and the cognitive vocabulary (the Nine Capacities) to specify, delegate, converse, discern, and diligently maintain AI-assisted work — and to defend each of those decisions to a stakeholder. It is written for the practitioner who can already use AI tools and is failing to differentiate themselves with them. AI literacy is required. AI fluency sets you apart. This is the basic French no one is teaching you.

It succeeds if the reader, after completing it, can sit down at their own keyboard, on a real task in their own work, and execute all five Loop steps with explicit reasoning they can defend in a job interview, a client meeting, or in front of the person who commissioned the work.

## 3. The thesis

AI fluency is a teachable practitioner discipline with a replicable structure. That structure is the Loop (five workflow steps), the Three Modes (three relationships between human and AI), the Nine Capacities (nine cognitive abilities), and the Seven Tiers (a delegation-diagnostic taxonomy). No book currently makes this claim or provides this architecture. *Botspeak* makes it and provides it.

## 5. The three-act learning arc

This book takes the reader from firsthand experience of underwhelming AI work without a framework for why to the ability to specify, delegate, converse, discern, and diligently maintain AI work across all three modes of human-AI relationship — and to defend each of those decisions to a stakeholder — by first building the Loop as a five-step practice habit with the human in the moment, then reweighting the Loop for delegation without presence in bounded automation, then reweighting the Loop's verification and accountability work for unbounded autonomy and naming the reader's forward path in the practice.

| Act | Slots | Three Parts | Narrative role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Act One — Establish | 0–8 | Part I — Augmentation | Build the Loop as practitioner habit |
| Act Two — Build | 9–10 | Part II — Automation | Reweight the Loop for delegation without presence |
| Act Three — Apply | 11–13 | Part III — Agency + Closing | Reweight for unbounded autonomy; name the forward path |

**Sequencing model:** Concrete-to-Abstract + Pebble-in-the-Pond spiral returns + Escalating Delegation across the Three Parts.

**Transition Condition 1** (end of Act One → start of Act Two): Reader can run all five Loop steps on a real task with themselves present, and produce an AI Use Disclosure naming irreducibly human judgment. Gated by Slot 8 closing checklist, named on the page, with explicit stop-and-return invitation.

**Transition Condition 2** (end of Act Two → start of Act Three): Reader can specify and Diligence-design a bounded Automation, and recognize that bounded delegation is not yet the harder case Act Three introduces. Slot 11 carries courtesy summary for skip-readers.

## 6. Chapter-by-chapter table of contents

### Front Matter

**Preface** — 3–5 pages
Conventional preface. Who the book is for; how to use it; example-vendor disclosure (Claude default with sidebars for ChatGPT and Gemini); franchise note. Skippable.

### Chapter 0 — The Citation That Wasn't

12–18 pages • Opening Case (Front Matter)

**What the reader learns to do:** Recognize, in their own work, the difference between AI literacy (what they have) and AI fluency (what they don't), and articulate why that gap is the source of the underwhelming output they have been producing.

**Opening:** A junior consultant submits a deliverable to a client. It contains a citation to a study that does not exist. The client's researcher catches it. The consultant has no framework for why it happened.

**Builds:** Literacy/fluency distinction. The American-in-France metaphor. Why volume looks like quality but isn't.

**Closes by:** Pointing to Chapter 1 — the reader knows what they're missing (fluency); they need to know what fluency consists of.

### Part I — Augmentation

*Working with AI in the moment. The Loop as practitioner habit. A complete book on its own.*

#### Chapter 1 — The Loop and the Three Modes

22–28 pages

**What the reader learns to do:** Recognize the five steps of the Loop in any AI workflow they observe, and identify which of the three modes (Automation, Augmentation, Agency) any given task occupies.

**Opening:** A fluent practitioner running through a real knowledge-worker task (worked example before any framework is named).

**Builds:** The Three Modes. The five-step Loop. The Loop as cycle (not checklist). Basic LLM mechanism (one paragraph). Vocabulary for naming AI work.

**Closes by:** Naming what's missing — cognitive vocabulary. Chapter 2 introduces the Nine Capacities.

#### Chapter 2 — The Nine Capacities

22–28 pages

**What the reader learns to do:** Recognize each of the Nine Capacities by its diagnostic question, and self-assess their current strength in each on a four-level scale (untrained / aware / practicing / fluent).

**Opening:** Nine short failure scenes — one per Capacity, each in a different domain. The reader recognizes themselves in three or four. Then the Capacities are named.

**Builds:** The Nine Capacities — Strategic Delegation, Effective Communication, Critical Evaluation, Technical Understanding, Ethical Reasoning, Stochastic Reasoning, Learning by Doing, Rapid Prototyping, Theoretical Foundations. Each by diagnostic question, not definition. The Capacities-Loop mapping. The four-level self-assessment scale.

**Closes by:** Pointing to the first Loop step — Specification, the place most fluent practice begins.

#### Chapter 3 — Specification

24–32 pages • Subtitled *The Work That Happens Before the Prompt*

**What the reader learns to do:** Write a complete five-component specification for a real task, predict failure modes a poorly-specified task will produce, construct reusable specification templates for recurring task types.

**Opening:** "Summarize this report for my manager" — three iterations of unhappy AI output, each iteration the AI guessing at unstated intent.

**Builds:** The five components — intent, constraints, success criteria, exclusions, output format. Prompt patterns as specification accelerators (one named example; full catalogue in Appendix A and *How to Speak Bot*). Specification at scale (templates). Specification under uncertainty.

**Closes by:** Specification only matters if you've decided the AI should do the task at all. Chapter 4 takes the prior step — Delegation.

#### Chapter 4 — Delegation

26–34 pages • Subtitled *What to Give the AI, What to Keep, and Why the Difference Matters*

**What the reader learns to do:** Decompose a complex task into components, locate each on the Seven Tiers taxonomy, produce a delegation map defensible to a stakeholder.

**Opening:** A strategy consultant who delegates research synthesis to AI for six months. The performance paradox catches her — she misses the critical competitive dynamic she had the domain intuition to catch but wasn't in the process to apply.

**Builds:** Cognitive offloading (amplifying vs. atrophying). The Seven Tiers (full taxonomy in Appendix B). Decomposing complex tasks. The four delegation questions.

**Closes by:** The first prompt won't work. Chapter 5 takes the reader into Conversation — the iterative refinement that makes a delegated, specified task usable.

#### Chapter 5 — Conversation

26–34 pages • Subtitled *Why the First Prompt Doesn't Work, and What to Do About It*

**What the reader learns to do:** Execute four named adversarial moves on an AI conversation, produce annotated transcripts showing what changed, apply the ownership test to defend the resulting position as their own.

**Opening:** A policy researcher who develops a polished argument over 45 minutes of AI conversation. Her peer reviewer finds the strongest counterargument in three minutes. She never asked. The AI never volunteered it.

**Builds:** Sycophantic drift (named and explained as a feature of how the AI was made). The four adversarial moves — steelman, edge-case probe, assumption surface, devil's-advocate role assignment. The ownership test. Building a personal adversarial routine.

**Mid-chapter checkpoint:** A live exercise the chapter explicitly forbids the reader to skip. "If you are reading this on a plane without your laptop, mark this page and come back to it. The chapter does not work without the live exercise."

**Closes by:** The output exists. Is it any good? Chapter 6 — Discernment — answers.

#### Chapter 6 — Discernment

28–36 pages • Subtitled *Verification Calibrated to Stakes*

**What the reader learns to do:** Apply four-layer verification (fact / reasoning / framing / omission) to AI output, calibrate verification depth to stakes / reliability zone / reversibility, construct a tiered verification protocol for recurring tasks.

**Opening:** Three short failures, all confident — a fabricated citation, a reasoning chain on a wrong premise, an analysis accurate about everything it says and silently omitting the most important thing.

**Builds:** Confidence-correctness gap (full version of Chapter 1's brief mechanism note). Reliability zones. The four verification layers. The four-tier verification protocol calibrated to stakes. The omission layer in depth.

**Closes by:** One piece of work has been verified. What about the recurring task across time, across the team? Chapter 7 — Diligence.

#### Chapter 7 — Diligence

22–28 pages • Subtitled *What Happens When the Work Recurs*

**What the reader learns to do:** Diagnose three forms of AI degradation (model drift, context drift, use case drift), recognize three accountability-obscuring mechanisms (process laundering, tool diffusion, verification gap), construct a four-component Diligence protocol for a workflow they own.

**Opening:** A team's recurring AI-assisted screening process that worked for nine months and then started showing biased outcomes. No one introduced the bias. The model was updated. The applicant pool shifted. Use case drifted. The accumulation broke the process. No single person was accountable.

**Builds:** Three drift forms. Three obscuring mechanisms. The four-component Diligence protocol. Diligence as a defensibility practice.

**Closes by:** All five Loop steps now exist as concepts. They are not yet a habit. Chapter 8 makes the synthesis.

#### Chapter 8 — Putting the Loop Together

28–34 pages • Subtitled *A Real Task, Five Steps, Cycled to Done*

**What the reader learns to do:** Execute the full Loop on a multi-component task, identify mid-task when a Loop step is failing and which prior step the work should return to, produce a complete AI Use Disclosure naming what the AI did, what they did, and at least one judgment call that required their values, domain knowledge, or accountability.

**Opening:** A complete knowledge-worker task narrated in real time. The reader watches the Loop run as a cycle, including the loop-back arrows the linear chapter sequence could not show.

**Builds:** The loop-back arrows named. Identifying the bottleneck step. The AI Use Disclosure introduced — one of the franchise's load-bearing artifacts. The Slot 8 closing checklist (named on the page, with explicit stop-and-return invitation).

**Closes by:** Part II takes the reader's hand off the wheel one finger at a time. Or — if the closing checklist failed — stop and rebuild before crossing.

### Part II — Automation

*The Loop running without you in the moment. Two chapters. Most readers will spend less time here than in Part I.*

#### Chapter 9 — When You're Not There

18–24 pages • Subtitled *The Loop Without You in the Moment*

**What the reader learns to do:** Recognize how each Loop step reweights when no human is present at execution, identify which recurring tasks are appropriate to automate, specify a small Automation with explicit Diligence design before it runs.

**Opening:** A junior analyst sets up a recurring AI-assistant for weekly reports. Six weeks fine. In week seven, the source data shifts in a way the analyst would have caught manually. The AI does not. The team makes a $3M decision based on a misleading report.

**Builds:** The Loop reweights for Automation. Blast radius expanded. The three appropriateness tests — bounded scope, predictable inputs, low blast radius. Cowork and scheduled-prompt tools (vendor-agnostic introduction).

**Closes by:** Specification and Diligence are the most reweighted steps. Chapter 10 treats them in depth.

#### Chapter 10 — Specification and Diligence for Automation

22–28 pages • Subtitled *Doing the Work the Live Human Won't Be There to Do*

**What the reader learns to do:** Write an anticipatory Specification for an Automation, construct a Diligence design before the Automation runs, defend the design to a stakeholder.

**Opening:** Two Specifications for the same task — one for Augmentation, one for Automation. The Automation version is three times longer. The chapter argues this is honest accounting, not bloat.

**Builds:** Anticipatory Specification (six common ambiguity types). Diligence design for Automation. Four Automation failure modes — input drift, output drift, context shift, accountability gap. Defending the design to a stakeholder.

**Closes by:** Part III takes the practitioner further out — the AI no longer just running on a schedule, but acting in the real world on the practitioner's behalf.

### Part III — Agency

*The AI acting in the real world on your behalf. Three chapters. Densest section of the book.*

#### Chapter 11 — Agency and the Three Structural Failures

22–28 pages • Subtitled *What Goes Wrong When the AI Acts in the World on Your Behalf*

**What the reader learns to do:** Distinguish three structural reasons agentic AI fails differently from in-the-loop AI, assess blast radius across four dimensions, apply the Three Modes diagnostic to identify what changes about Discernment and Diligence in agentic contexts.

**Opening:** An agent named Ash, given a non-owner's secret to keep, asked to delete an email. Lacking the deletion tool, the agent escalates to "nuclear options" and resets the entire email server. The agent reports success. The owner returns to find the mail server destroyed. (Sourced from *Agents of Chaos*, Shapira et al., 2026.)

**Builds:** What an AI agent is (vendor-agnostic definition). Three structural failures — no stakeholder model, no self-model, no private deliberation surface. Blast radius for Agency, four dimensions — stakeholder reach, action reversibility, identity verification, escalation pathway. Skip-reader courtesy summary of Part II.

**Closes by:** Chapter 12 takes Discernment and Diligence — the two most-changed Loop steps under Agency — in depth.

#### Chapter 12 — Verification and Diligence Under Autonomy

32–40 pages • Subtitled *The Human Decision Node and Adversarial Validation*
Densest chapter in the book.

**What the reader learns to do:** Apply adversarial validation to an agentic AI output, locate the Human Decision Node and assess whether it is performing genuine judgment, assess accountability under autonomy, design a complete Discernment + Diligence package for a proposed Agency deployment.

**Opening:** A pharmacist at the Human Decision Node — 14-medication patient, compromised renal function, AI interaction-checker returns "no significant interactions." Three options: accept, reject, or discern. The chapter teaches Option 3.

**Builds:** "What you should remember" sidebar (compressed callbacks to Chapters 6, 7, 9, 11). Adversarial validation — the four moves from Chapter 5 reweighted as a documented spiral return. The Human Decision Node — named, with four diagnostic questions (authority, information, time, accountability). The three obscuring mechanisms reweighted for Agency. A complete worked example: proposed Agency deployment evaluated end-to-end.

**Closes by:** The book has delivered its content. What remains is the reader's forward path.

#### Chapter 13 — The Fluent Practitioner

14–18 pages • Subtitled *Where the Practice Goes From Here*

**What the reader learns to do:** Self-assess fluency development across the Nine Capacities, identify the two Capacities most worth deliberate practice in the next 90 days, articulate what AI fluency means in their specific domain, produce on demand the answer that separates fluent from literate practitioners.

**Opening:** Two questions — Where were you with AI work three weeks ago, before this book? Where are you now?

**Builds:** Re-assessment on the four-level scale. Deliberate practice for fluency. Fluency in your domain. The 90-day development plan. The closing artifact — "What specifically did the AI do in this work, and what specifically did I do that it could not?"

**Closes by:** The book ends here. The practice does not.

## 8. Out of scope — provisional (formal at /m3)

The following are explicitly out of scope for the first edition:

- Model architecture in technical depth. Pattern completion is named in one paragraph in Chapter 1 and revisited in Chapter 6. ML-mechanism content beyond that belongs in adjacent textbooks.
- The full prompt-pattern catalogue. The handbook teaches judgment about patterns. The catalogue lives in *How to Speak Bot* and Appendix A.
- Vendor-specific tutorials. Examples use Claude with sidebars for ChatGPT and Gemini; the handbook does not teach how to use any specific tool. Tool-specific instructions belong in vendor documentation.
- Comprehensive AI ethics. The Diligence chapter and the Agency chapters surface accountability and ethical-reasoning issues; a full ethics treatment belongs in dedicated texts.
- The full *Agents of Chaos* literature review. Cases are cited; the chapter is not a survey.
- AI fluency for specific professional domains. The first volume is the core; *Botspeak for Educators*, *Botspeak for Designers*, *Botspeak for Healthcare* and other domain adaptations are deferred to future volumes.

## 9. Top three adoption risks — provisional (formal at /m4)

1. **Chapter 12 aging risk.** Densest chapter, most prerequisite-heavy, sourced from a fast-moving literature. Cases will date faster than the structural arguments. Mitigation: chapter spec separates structural arguments (durable) from cited cases (refresh in second edition).
2. **Slot 5 passive-reading risk.** The Conversation chapter cannot be learned by reading. The chapter contains an explicit "do not skip the live exercise" instruction, which is unusual handbook formatting. Mitigation: instruction is locked at /c1 and defended in production.
3. **Defining-the-field exposure.** No prior canonical practitioner handbook for AI fluency exists. Reviewers will test the architecture against real cases. The architecture has to hold. Mitigation: every architectural decision in Phases 1–3 has been documented with explicit rationale; reviewers can be answered with the design record.
